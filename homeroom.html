Homeroom: A Virtual Collaboration Tool

Type

Class project, web app

Role

User Research, UI Design

Duration

Aug - Dec 2020 (3 months)

Teammates

Devanei Solai, Rebecca Tafete

Description

An online collaboration tool for group activities in virtual classes (late elementary to early high school)

The problem

This year, a lot of students and teachers have been thrown into virtual learning with little prior training or notice. Because of the transition, a lot of changes have been made to the way classes operate. Collaboration is an integral tool in classrooms, especially when students and teachers are living more isolated lives, but current technologies for virtual learning don't allow for the same collaborative experience that students get in classrooms. We wanted to create a tool that improves on virtual collaboration and makes it as easy and beneficial as in-person collaboration.

User research

To create a helpful classroom tool, we had to get a better understanding of our main stakeholders: students, teachers, and administrators. We observed 4, interviewed 5, and surveyed 62 people across these stakeholder categories, focusing on comparing their online and in-person classroom experiences. We also did competitive analysis on a few video conferencing and collaboration platforms and noted their strengths and shortcomings.

A couple graphs from our survey results and an affinity diagram from our user research and competitive analysis notes are shown below. We used the affinity diagram to organize and identify our key findings.

Key findings

Advantages of virtual collaboration

Some advantages of virtual collaboration include the flexibility of meetups and the convenience of digital collaboration.

Limited communication

Reduced communication among teachers and students makes collaboration harder in a virtual classroom.


Changing needs

Students have different emotional, social, and technological needs during quarantine.

Digital proficiency

Teachers need to be proficient with virtual learning tools before using them to implement collaboration in classrooms.

Understanding our stakeholders

Based on the people we researched, we created three user personas highlighting the backgrounds, frustrations, and goals of our stakeholder groups during the transition to online learning.


Redefining our stakeholders

To create a more targeted solution, we decided to limit our stakeholder group to middle schoolers (± a few years). This was a good age group as many of our survey participants were middle school teachers and students, who often do small collaborative tasks in class.

Developing three different ideas

Based on our research, we established three design criteria that we needed to address in our product. We then brainstormed a list of 100+ product ideas that addressed these criteria, and took three of our favorite ideas and drafted them into lo-fi wireframes. Each idea we chose focuses on one of the design criteria.



Technology

Our product should take advantage of virtual learning by including various collaborative features to enhance group work.

Communication

Our product must allow teachers to effectively communicate with students and monitor student collaboration.

Learnability

Our product must be easy to learn and require little prior training to use.


Combining ideas + Wireframes

We decided to take the notable features of each idea and combined them into one design. We created two user flows outlining how a student and a teacher may potentially use our product, and drew out wireframes of our ideas.

Improvements and future steps

To improve Homeroom, my team performed a cognitive walkthrough exercise, where we went through the main interactions users would have with our product and noted any learnability issues. We found a few issues with confusing buttons and other UI features and proposed ways to fix these issues. A list of the learnability issues we detected can be found here.

In addition, we also prepared documents that we would use for user testing, including a script, a consent form, and a task list for participants. If we have the opportunity in the future, we would use these documents to get feedback on how to improve our product. What I learned

As a class project, Homeroom gave me the chance to practice user research and UI design in a structured environment, with strict deadlines but also with the guidance of my professor and TA. I went through an organized empathize-define-ideate-prototype-test process and took on a variety of roles in the process.

In particular, I enjoyed delving more into user research through this project. My experience with observing, surveying, and interviewing our stakeholders taught me not only a lot about our project topic but also about my personal experiences with virtual learning. I was also able to learn a lot about the technicalities of user research, from creating consent forms to learning about the limitations of researching minors.

Overall, I really enjoyed working on this project! I had really amazing teammates and we had a fun time working with each other. It was cool to be able to explore current issues with remote education and think outside the box to come up with a solution.
