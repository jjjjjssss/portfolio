Homeroom: A Virtual Collaboration Tool

Teammates

Bridging the gap between online and in-person collaboration

With the shift to virtual education during the pandemic, my team and I decided to create a tool that improves virtual collaboration experiences in classrooms.


The problem

This year, a lot of students and teachers have been thrown into virtual learning with little prior training or notice. Because of the transition, a lot of changes have been made to the way classes operate. Collaboration is an integral tool in classrooms, especially when students and teachers are living more isolated lives, but current technologies for virtual learning don't allow for the same collaborative experience that students get in classrooms.

User research

To get a better understanding of the problem at hand, we observed 4, interviewed 5, and surveyed 62 people across three stakeholder categories—students, teachers, and administrators—on their experiences with virtual collaboration. We also did competitive analysis on various video conferencing and collaboration platforms. Here are a few quotes that reflect our findings.

Survey results and affinity diagram

A couple graphs from our survey results and an affinity diagram from our user research and competitive analysis notes are shown below. We used the affinity diagram to organize and identify our key findings.

Key findings

  • Some advantages of virtual collaboration include the flexibility of meetups and the convenience of digital collaboration.
  • Reduced communication among teachers and students makes collaboration harder in a virtual classroom.
  • Students have different emotional, social, and technological needs during quarantine.
  • Teachers need to be proficient with virtual learning tools before using them to implement collaboration in classrooms.

User personas

We created three user personas highlighting the backgrounds, frustrations, and goals of the stakeholders we researched in their transitions to online learning.



To create a more targeted solution, we decided to limit our student stakeholder group to middle schoolers (± a few years). This was a good age group as many of our survey participants were middle school teachers and students, who often do small collaborative tasks in class.

Developing three different ideas

Based on our research, we established three design criteria to address in our product. We then brainstormed 100+ product ideas that addressed these criteria, and took three of our favorite ideas and drafted them into lo-fi wireframes.

Criteria 1: Technology

Our product should take advantage of virtual learning by including various collaborative features to enhance group work.

Proposed solution: "Applying Teamwork Strategies," which incorporates different collaborative tools like whiteboards and to-do lists

Criteria 2: Communication

Our product must allow teachers to effectively communicate with students and monitor student collaboration.

Proposed solution: "Classroom Control," which has a side panel where students can request teacher help

Criteria 3: Learnability

Our product must be easy to learn and require little prior training to use.

Proposed solution: "Mimicking In-Person Classroom Design," which features a classroom layout of groups easy for users to understand

Combining ideas + Wireframes

We decided to take the notable features of each idea and combine them into one design. We created two user flows outlining how a student and a teacher may potentially use our product, and drew out wireframes of our ideas.

The solution

Our mockups have a similar structure as the wireframes, but improve on some buttons and other features in the sidebar. For the visual design, we wanted something simple and monochromatic, so we decided on a blue color scheme against a light-colored background.

Improvements and future steps

To improve Homeroom, my team performed a cognitive walkthrough exercise, where we noted any learnability issues users may experience with our product. We found a few issues with confusing buttons and other UI features and proposed ways to fix them. A list of the learnability issues we detected can be found here.

In addition, we also prepared documents that we would use for user testing, including a script, a consent form, and a task list for participants. If we have the opportunity in the future, we would use these documents to get feedback on how to improve our product. What I learned

As a class project, Homeroom gave me the chance to practice user research and UI design in a structured environment, with strict deadlines but also with the guidance of my professor and TA. I went through an organized empathize-define-ideate-prototype process and took on a variety of roles in the process.

In particular, I enjoyed delving more into user research through this project. My experience with observing, surveying, and interviewing our stakeholders taught me not only a lot about our project topic but also helped me reflect on my personal experiences with virtual learning.

Overall, I really enjoyed working on this project! I had really amazing teammates and we had a fun time working with each other.
