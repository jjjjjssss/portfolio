Overview

GT Scheduler: Improving the course registration process

Course registration is a stressful experience for many students at Georgia Tech, exacerbated by the school’s outdated course registration tools. As a result, many students rely on third-party scheduling tools, such as GT Scheduler, developed by former student Jason Park.

To help maintain this app, my team of 12 at Bits of Good was tasked to make improvements and add new features to the scheduler.

Existing features

Currently, GT Scheduler features two main pages:

A calendar view to plan out weekly class times.
A map view to see where the locations of classes compare to each other.

New features by our team

While GT Scheduler currently provides basic tools for schedule planning, our team wanted to add new features to further streamline this process. Over the semester, we worked on three new features:

A course catalog with advanced searching and filtering capabilities and detailed information on each course.
A feedback widget to share ideas, feedback, or questions about the scheduler.
The ability to create multiple schedules for one semester so students can try out different schedule plans.
Feature 1
Overview

1. Course Catalog: Overview

The primary feature our team worked on was the course catalog, where students can search for courses and view detailed course information. This enables students to not only plan schedules but also research the courses they want to take.

A centralized space for course details

The course catalog consolidates essential course information, such as:

  • a course description,
  • prerequisites,
  • GPA breakdowns for the entire course and individual professors,
  • the number of seats available in a course's sections and waitlists, and
  • location and times of each section.

Advanced searching and filtering capabilities

To make it easier to find the specific course students want, the catalog has an advanced filtering system to filter by credit hours, class times, delivery mode, and campus location.

User research

Identifying user needs

The idea of a course catalog came from the previous team that worked on GT Scheduler, who observed that students cross-referenced 2-3 different resources while scheduling. To learn more, my design partner and I interviewed six Georgia Tech students about their registration experience.

Preferences of a typical student

Based on the interviews, we constructed an archetype of how a typical student approaches registration.

Registration Process: The student typically has a flexible 4-year plan laid out for core courses, but is willing to explore different electives. When choosing courses, they prioritize:

  • good professors
  • class style and difficulty (such as the average course GPA and the professor’s teaching style)
  • course prerequisites

Tools and Functions: Common tools they use for registration include:

  • OSCAR: the official Georgia Tech registration platform
  • Course Critique: displays the historical GPA for each course and each professor
  • Rate My Professors/Reddit: places where students leave reviews about their professors
  • DegreeWorks: where students go to see if their courses meet the requirements for their major

User needs

From these interviews, we also identified four main needs that our course catalog should fulfill.

Students should be able to view basic information like course prerequisites, number of credit hours, and class location.
Students should have information to help them choose between course sections based on professor difficulty and class times.
Students should be able to easily identify when courses have conflicting times.
Students should be able to filter for courses based on time, location, and other preferences.
Secondary Research

Drawing from related tools

We also analyzed common registration tools brought up in the user interviews and reached out to students at other schools to recommend their favorite schedulers.

Keeping user needs in mind, we picked out features from each of these tools to incorporate into our course catalog.

OSCAR → Course sections table

OSCAR is the official Georgia Tech platform for registration, where they can view detailed information about course sections. However, the outdated interface and confusing navigation are frustrating for students to use.

Course Critique → Average GPAs

Course Critique compiles the historical course GPAs of all Georgia Tech courses and professors. Many students rely on this to evaluate the difficulty of their courses.

Penn Course Plan → Search and filter

Penn Course Plan is a student-created scheduler for the University of Pennsylvania. This scheduler not has an effective search and filtering tool, but also condenses a course catalog and calendar view on one screen.

QuACS → Course description

Like Penn Course Plan, QuACS is a student-created scheduler for Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute. It has a separate course catalog that lists out course descriptions and course sections, and courses can be added to the calendar view directly via the catalog.

The drawing board

Wireframes

With a better understanding of potential features for the catalog, we started brainstorming ideas for the page’s layout. We explored three types of ways to integrate the catalog into the scheduler.

User Flow

To clarify to ourselves and our developers how users would navigate through the catalog, we created and updated a user flow throughout this project, with a focus on searching and filtering.

User Feedback

A second opinion

Do we need a separate course catalog page?

While designing the wireframes and user flow, our team started having some doubts—we felt that course information could be best displayed as a popup in the current calendar view without the need for a separate course catalog.

We asked users from the previous interviews on their thoughts. They still preferred the separate course catalog, because they saw themselves using the calendar view and catalog view for two distinct tasks: organizing class times versus learning about courses.

Wireframes

Iterations

With the user feedback in mind, we continuously refined our designs for a standalone catalog page. We sought feedback on our designs through weekly design critiques with other designers in Bits of Good and a mid-semester demo to the organization’s product team.

Mockups

The final solution

Through multiple iterations, we refined our design to meet the user needs we identified at the user research phase.

Students should be able to view basic information like course prerequisites, number of credit hours, and class location.

This information is condensed into tables that reflect the familiar interface of Georgia Tech’s official registration platform, but we eliminated unimportant columns and made the UI more scannable.

Students should have information to help them choose between course sections based on professor difficulty and class times.

The page displays historical GPAs and grade breakdowns for entire courses and specific professors. The catalog's table view makes it easy to compare class times.

Students should be able to easily identify when courses have conflicting times.

Course sections that conflict with a student’s current schedule are grayed out.

Students should be able to search and filter for courses based on time, location, and other preferences.

Users can expand the search bar to access specific search filters like class times, credit hours, delivery mode, and campus location.

Looking forward

Future features for the catalog

The catalog designs successfully tackle the basic needs of researching courses. In order to make this an even more comprehensive platform for course registration, our team brainstormed some future features for the catalog.

Based on other tools students use for course registration:

Displaying professor reviews and ratings by students from Rate My Professors
Incorporate a syllabi uploading and sharing functionality

Other features:

A course tracking and notification system for students to receive updates on course and waitlist availability
User accounts that enable students to enter their major and previous courses to see if they have fulfilled prerequisites and highlight courses they still need to take
Feature 2

2. Feedback Widget

Previously, to collect feedback on GT Scheduler, our team relied on a combination of Google Analytics, Google Forms, and word of mouth. We looked into a more direct way to collect this feedback.

Ideation

We identified two key inputs for the feedback widget: one rating system to measure the scheduler's performance, and one text input for comments. We mocked up a few ways to display these fields.

Final design

Users can select the feedback button on the bottom right of the scheduler page to open up the feedback form.

Not long after we implemented the feedback form, we received 30+ responses from students, including feature suggestions, bug reports, and pain points we could use in future improvements to the scheduler.

Feature 3

3. Multiple schedules

A lot of students had requested for the ability to create multiple schedules for their alternative plans. We added a small functionality for creating, deleting, and renaming new schedules.

Reflection

Closing thoughts

This project was one of my first times working in a cross-functional team. While I mostly just worked with the other designer in the beginning, I began to collaborate more with the product manager to discuss user needs and product requirements, and with the engineering manager and developers to understand the feasibility of our designs.

This project helped me gain a better understanding of my role as a designer—the bridge between product and engineering—and helped prepare me for my first internship that summer. I definitely grew a lot working on a real-world project with a direct impact on the students around me.
